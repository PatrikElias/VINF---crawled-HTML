    The First Battle of Kiev was the German name for the operation that resulted in a huge encirclement of Soviet troops in the vicinity of Kiev during World War II. This encirclement is considered the largest encirclement in the history of warfare (by number of troops). The operation ran from 7 July to 26 September 1941, as part of Operation Barbarossa, the Axis invasion of the Soviet Union.

    Much of the Southwestern Front of the Red Army (commanded by Mikhail Kirponos) was encircled, but small groups of Red Army troops managed to escape the pocket days after the German panzers met east of the city, including the headquarters of Marshal Semyon Budyonny, Marshal Semyon Timoshenko and Commissar Nikita Khrushchev. Kirponos was trapped behind German lines and was killed while trying to break out.

    The battle was an unprecedented defeat for the Red Army, exceeding even the Battle of Białystok–Minsk of June–July 1941. The encirclement trapped 452,700 soldiers, 2,642 guns and mortars, and 64 tanks, of which scarcely 15,000 soldiers had escaped from the encirclement by 2 October. The Southwestern Front suffered 700,544 casualties, including 616,304 killed, captured, or missing during the battle. The 5th, 37th, 26th, 21st, and 38th armies, consisting of 43 divisions, were almost annihilated and the 40th Army suffered many losses. Like the Western Front before it, the Southwestern Front had to be recreated almost from scratch.

    Despite the given name of "Battle of Kiev", the city of Kiev itself played a small and peripheral role in the conflicts, and the battle took place in a large area in eastern Ukraine.

    Adolf Hitler, the leader of the Third Reich, described the Battle of Kiev as "the greatest battle in the history of the world".[6]

    Prelude[edit]

    Plans and preparations[edit]

    According to Barbarossa's operational plan, the Wehrmacht's Army Group South under the command of Field Marshal Gerd von Rundstedt was supposed to dominate the strategic area of Ukraine. This force should have reached the Dnieper river in an enveloping movement by breaking the Soviet border defense and penetrating 650 kilometers deep into its territory and encircle the main forces of the Red Army in Ukraine west of this river.[7]

    On the other hand, the Soviet High Command knew that the main focus of German forces in their invasion would be north of the Pripyat marshes, but Soviet leader Joseph Stalin insisted that the Soviet emphasis should be on the south.[8] The Soviet Southwestern Front under the command of General Mikhail Kirponos in Ukraine as the most powerful front in the western borders of the Soviet Union,[9] had a total of four field armies with the support of three mechanized corps in the front line, one mechanized corps in reserve and two mechanized corps in strategic reserve position.[10]

    In this situation, the Pripyat marshes which separated southern part of the operational area of the German forces from its northern part, had eliminated the possibility of receiving help for the army group south from other German units until after the Kiev area.[11] On the other hand, the operational area in Ukraine had a greater depth and width than the operational areas of the Wehrmacht's other two army groups, and Kiev 600 km away from the border line, was considered a farther objective. These issues made the responsibility of the army group south more difficult.[12]

    June and July[edit]

    After start of Operation Barbarossa on June 22, 1941, Army Group South attacked Ukraine.[13] The Red Army forces in this area put up a strong defense and despite the serious lack of fuel and ammunition, were obliged by the high command to carry out immediate counter-attacks.[14] However, the 13th and 14th panzer divisions captured Lutsk on June 26. In this situation, Panzer Group 1 as the armored spearhead of Army Group South was thought to be to in an ideal position to advance towards Kiev via Rivne. Meanwhile, Kirponos was trying to mount a concentrated counterattack. Commencement of his counter-attack on June 26 led to an armored battle of unprecedented scale between more than 2,000 tanks on a 70-kilometer front line.[15] These fragmented counter-attacks due to the inexperience of the Red Army officers in controlling the mass of armored forces,[10] failed to achieve its objectives and resulted in destruction of many Soviet reserves in the region.[16] Nevertheless, These intense battles also caused many losses on German side. Thus, due to these border skirmishes in June, Army Group South lagged behind the rest of the German forces in the advance.[14]

    Finally, on June 30, Lviv was captured by the German 17th Army.[17] On the same day, after proving the ineffectiveness of counter-attacks, Stavka ordered Kirponos to withdraw his forces to new defensive positions in the fortified areas of Korosten, Novgorod-Volynski and Letychiv along the 1939 Soviet-Polish border, known as the Stalin Line by July 9. Despite the delay in the advance of the German III Motorized Corps with the Kirponos counterattack and the provision of more time for his forces to retreat to the designated defensive positions,[18] by the arrival of the 11th Panzer Division at Berdichev on July 7, Stalin line was also broken and a gap was created between the Soviet Fifth and Sixth Armies south of Novgorod-Volynski.[17]

    With Kiev under threat and the risk of the southwestern front split in half between the Soviet 5th Army in Kiev and its 6th Army south of Berdychiv, Stavka ordered a new series of counter-attacks on the same day to destroy the enemy spearhead, stop the advance of the Germans towards Kiev and maintain communication between the these two armies. However, before these counterattacks began between July 11 and 14,[19] after the capture of Zhitomir by German forces on July 8,[20] With the weakening of the defense line of the Red Army, German III Motorized Corps managed to break through the enemy's defensive line and open the road to Kiev on July 10.[21] On the same day, 13th Panzer Division of the III Motorized Corps by taking advantage of the created gap known as the "Zhitomir Corridor" reached the Irpen River, just 15 kilometers west of Kiev.[22] Heavy clashes resulted from the Red Army counterattacks again caused a lot of damage to the mobile forces of the Soviet Southwestern Front; In a way that only 7 BT and 25 T-26 tanks of the 9th Mechanized Corps, only 4 KV-1, 7 T-34 and 22 T-26 tanks of the 19th Mechanized Corps and only 2 BT and 28 T-26 tanks of the 22nd Mechanized Corps remained out of their initial strength.[23] Meanwhile, Due to the 100 kilometers distance of the following German infantry and artillery, needed to assault Kiev, from the front line, the decision to attack quickly to the city was left to the higher levels of German command. At this time, Nikita Khrushchev, the political commissar of the Soviet Southwestern Front, was in charge of the Kiev garrison, including three rifle divisions, an airborne brigade, a tank regiment, some motorized forces of NKVD, students of the 1st Artillery College of Kiev, two anti-tank battalions, and about 29,000 militiamen.[22]

    Debates over the attack on Kiev[edit]

    On July 9, Army Group South command and the German Army High Command (OKH) re-emphasized the encirclement and destruction of the soviet forces west of the Dnieper as the main objective of the operation. In any case, seeing the imminent collapse of the Soviet South-West Front, capturing of Kiev was also considered as a secondary goal. Nevertheless, At this time Hitler did not want to assault Kiev and saw the achievement of a bridgehead on the Dnieper subject to the destruction of large formations of soviet forces west of that river. However, German Army senior commanders were still interested in capturing Kiev. Repeated attacks by the forces of the Soviet Fifth Army from the Pripyat region did not allow the use of the German Sixth Army for this purpose. Rundstedt was confident that Kiev would be captured by the III Motorized Corps in its rapid advance, but Hitler, after consulting with Field marshal Walter von Brauchitsch, commander in chief of the German army, felt that such a move would "unnecessarily sacrifice" panzer forces. Finally, at one o'clock in the morning on July 10, Generaloberst Franz Halder, chief of the German army general staff, called the headquarters of the Army Group South and prohibited the use of panzer forces in the direction of Kiev, except for reconnaissance and security purposes. By approving this order, Hitler made the III Motorized Corps only responsible for protection against attacks from Kiev direction and ordered to avoid assaulting the city by this corps. He further pointed out that if the encirclement of the soviet forces up to the southern Bug River is not achieved, the whole of the 1st Armored Group will advance towards Kiev and the Dnieper River in the southeast.[24]

    Meanwhile, Rundstedt believed that with the retreat of many units of the Red Army to the east, it was no longer practical to encircle them up to the southern Bug River, and together with Halder, he wanted to encircle them around Bila Tserkva, west of the Dnieper River, which Hitler expressed his consent provided that the northern flank of the Panzer Group 1 be protected. with ambiguity in Hitler's condition, Brauchich concluded that the capture of Kiev would secure the Panzer Group's northern flank. However, as in the previous approach, he left the final decision to the commander of the Army Group South. Meanwhile, Rundstedt and Field marshal Walter von Reichenau, commander of the German 6th Army, who had previously supported the assault on Kiev, now doubted it. Finally, Generaloberst Ewald von Kleist, commander of the Panzer Group 1, was advised that if his offensive in coordination with 6th Army provided a suitable opportunity to capture this city without the risk of being pushed back, the local commander is free to do so. Thus, after much concern at the top levels, the final decision was left to a low-ranking commander. Finally, the reconnaissance forces of the Wehrmacht determined on July 13 that the fortifications and concentration of the Soviet forces in Kiev left no chance for a surprise attack.[24]

    Change in German strategy[edit]

    In early July, German Army Group Center in continuation of the operation Barbarossa after the end of the battle of Smolensk, planned to attack Moscow from three axes in the beginning of September.[25] Nevertheless, in the meantime the Soviet Fifth Army was still holding its previous positions in the Pripyat Marshes until mid-July, and this caused the creation of an additional front line of more than 250 kilometers in the open southern and northern flanks of the German army groups Center and South. However, in fact pieces of defeated Red Army units without equipment, fuel and ammunition were scattered in this region.[26] In the face of this situation and following the strong resistance of the Soviet forces around Smolensk, German army High Command changed its strategy for the continuation of the operation from mid-July.[27] Hitler's directive No. 33 on July 19, while criticizing the performance of the northern flank of the Army Group South in front of the Soviet Fifth Army, in order to restore focus and clarify the priority of the objectives, mandated the German forces to "prevent escape of large amounts of enemy forces by crossing the Dnieper River deep into the Russian territories and destroy them". On this basis, the left wing of the Army Group South, in cooperation with the right wing of the Army Group Center, should destroy the Soviet Fifth Army. In this way, Army Group Center should continued advance towards Moscow only with its infantry.[28] In any case, no joint command was established for the parts of both army groups that were to cooperate in the new operation.[29]

    Soviet countermeasure[edit]

    In response to such German actions, Stavka formed the Soviet Central Front under the command of General Fyodor Kuznetsov, consisting of the severely weakened 13th and 21st Armies on July 23 to protect the Gomel and the Sozh River regions on the southern flank of the German Army Group Center. However, this force could hardly be able to accomplish such a task.[30]

    General Georgy Zhukov, the Chief of the General Staff of the Red Army, upon receiving information from the front line, concluded that the movement of the Army Group Center to the east has stopped and that the biggest strategic threat to the Soviet forces is in the Central front; Where the movement of German mechanized forces to the south could cause the Soviet Southwestern Front to be in danger of being encircled. Zhukov told Stalin on July 29 that the success of any German advance toward Leningrad and Moscow seemed unlikely, but that the Gomel region was the Red Army's key strategic weakness. Therefore, Zhukov recommended that three armies, including one army from the strategic reserve and two armies from the western and Soviet southwestern fronts, and larger amounts of artillery be assigned to the Soviet central front And Lt. Gen. Nikolai Vatutin be appointed as its commander. Stalin pessimistically stated that such an action by transferring forces will weaken vital Moscow axis. In response, Zhukov rejected this issue and announced that it is possible to transfer 9 fully equipped divisions from the Far East within two weeks to strengthen this axis. Stalin again expressed doubts that the Far East will be fall into the hands of the Japanese. Ignoring this doubts, Zhukov suggested that the Soviet Southwestern Front be pulled back behind the Dnieper River. When Stalin asked about the fate of Kiev in this case, Zhukov citing military reasons, explained that this city could be abandoned. Faced with this speech, Stalin became upset and rebuking Zhukov in a harsh tone and calling his words "worthless". He announced his intention to preserve Kiev at all costs, like Leningrad. Zhukov aggressively told Stalin that if he thought the Chief of the General Staff was talking "nonsense", he would be dismissed and sent to the front line. Stalin, who did not intend to back down, agreed to this request and appointed Marshal Boris Shaposhnikov to replace Zhukov as the chief of the Red Army General Staff and him to command the Soviet reserve front.[30]

    Continuation of Army Group south advance[edit]

    Despite the stubborn resistance of the Red Army forces and unexpectedly heavy rain, German 17th Army managed to break through in the Vinnytsia region, and its 11th Army advanced to the Dniester River. The conditions of the battlefields made the Panzer Group 1 obliged to reach the Dnieper River and create a bridgehead on it south of Kiev.[31] On July 28, Stavka ordered the Soviet southwestern and southern fronts in a joint operation to disrupt the German offensive and prevent them from reaching the Dnieper River. The Soviet Southwestern Front had to reorganize its forces and prepare to attack towards Radomyshl and Zhitomir to connect with the Soviet Southern Front.[32] However, Germans completed encirclement of soviet forces in Uman region on August 4 and the Soviet 6th and 12th Armies trapped in it. Therefore, previous Stavka order seemed redundant until and Thus the Soviet Fifth Army received the 1st Airborne Corps, the 15th and 31st Rifle Corps, and the 9th and 22nd Mechanized Corps to attack alone the same day. The counterattack of the Soviet Fifth Army was stopped on August 8 Anyway with little gains and heavy casualties. On the same day, Korosten, 150 kilometers northwest of Kiev, was captured by the German XVII Army Corps, so the Soviet Fifth Army forced to retreat towards Kiev.[33]

    Stavka on formed the 38th Soviet Army based on the 8th Mechanized Corps on August 4 under the command of Lieutenant General Dmitry Ryabyshev with all available units in the Cherkasy region in order to cover the gap created as a result of the loss of the Uman region armies.[34] This army with total strength of 40,000 troops in form of five rifle and four cavalry divisions, which mostly recentley arrived in the region, was tasked to defend a 200 km front line.[35] Stavka also formed the Soviet 37th Army under Major General Andrey Vlasov six days later, after the Soviet 6th and 12th Armies were officially disbanded, and stationed it on the line of the Dnieper River between Kiev and Kremenchuk.[34]

    The advance elements of the German 6th Army reached the outskirts of Kiev on August 6, but were stopped by the Soviet 37th Army. Two days later, the German 6th Army was forced to take a defensive position and was pushed back from some positions by the Soviet 37th Army's counterattack from August 12. On the same day, Hitler ordered Rundstedt to stop the assault on Kiev.[36]

    Rundstedt issued his Order No. 5 on August 10, among other things, ordering his forces to capture the west bank of the Dnieper River. According to this order, Nikolaev was captured by Germans on August 16, and SS panzer division Leibstandarte SS Adolf Hitler reached Kherson on August 19. Meanwhile, German 22nd Infantry Division of the III Army Corps captured a 650-metre bridge over the Dnieper in Breslav for the 11th Army. On the same day, 9th Panzer Division secured a crossing over the Dnieper in Zaporozhye. Finally, the 13th Panzer Division also managed to reach capture a 900-meter bridge in Dnipropetrovsk on August 25. German 60th Motorized Infantry Division next day, and 198th Infantry Division and SS Viking division within a week reached this position and consolidated the bridgehead. The heavy counterattacks of the Red Army in this position targeted the Germans from three sides, but the Wehrmacht forces with the support of the Luftwaffe aircraft, including the 22nd Italian fighter group, repelled them. Rundstedt tried to create another bridgehead in Cherkasy to reduce the pressure on the previous bridgeheads. With the success of this plan, marshal Semyon Budyonny, commander of the soviet Southwestern Direction, counter-attacked against Cherkasy.[37]

    Army Group Center turning to the south[edit]

    According to the July 29 report, out of initial total of 953 tanks of the Army Group Center's Panzer Group 2 at the beginning of the operation, only 263 tanks remained serviceable as of July 25. This unit also lost about 20,000 men of its initial strength of 113,000 troops up to this time, while had received only 10,000 soldiers as replacements. Therefore, Generaloberst Heintz Guderian, commander of the Panzer Group 2 in the right (southern) flank of the Army Group Center, Informed Army Group commander that his units would not be ready to undertake any new operations before August 5.[38] Thus, in the face of the high number of enemy forces and logistical problems, on July 30, the Hitler directive No. 34 asked Army Group Center, while taking a defensive posture in the best positions along with limited offensive operations, as soon as possible to pull its two Panzer Groups out the front line to rebuilt and refitt in order to transfer them to the flanks. In the context of the Germans realizing the true scale of the war, the purpose of such orders in stopping Army Group Center, in the face of increasing Soviet resistance in the Moscow axis, was to focus on Leningrad in the north and Kiev and Ukraine in the south.[39] In any case, the important advantage of such turning of Army Group Center to the south was that, considering the battlefield conditions and the bulge on the front line, a very large encirclement would be created if the Red Army forces did not evacuate the bulge in time; While the distance that the German motorized forces had to travel to close this encirclement was shorter than the previous ones, and this operation was more suited to the reduced combat strength and the poor logistical situation of the Germans. On the other hand, with the gradual change in the nature of the operation Barbarossa from a mean to inflict a hard blow on the Soviet Union that would cause its collapse in 1941 to a war of resources, the operation in the south promised more benefits considering the limited resources available to Germany.[40]

    Due to the remaining of German Army High Command emphasis and interest on Moscow, in order to preserve the necessary resources for the future advance to the east, initially the number of units assigned to the Panzer Group 2 for the new operation was limited.[41] By Halder's order, despite Guderian's opposition and protest, only units of the Panzer Group 2 were to be used, which "are not needed in other missions of the Army Group Center and have enough readiness and mobility for combat". In order to counter the Soviet Fifth Army, Halder simply ordered the capture of those parts of Gomel and Chernigov that could be reached.[42]

    By the beginning of the execution of Hitler's directive No. 34 on August 6 by the Central Army Group, German Second Army and the XXIV Motorized Corps of the Panzer Group 2 attacked in direction of Gomel in the south by crossing the Suzh River.[43] Guderian's forces with the air support of the 3rd and 53rd bomber, 210th heavy fighter and 51st fighters wings,[44] by pushing back the 21st Army of the Soviet Central Front to the south, threatening Red Army front line by creating a gap between its southern front around Kiev and the units of the western and reserve fronts east of Smolensk. Stavka tried on August 14 by forming Bryansk Front under the command of Lieutenant General Andrey Yeryomenko with Soviet 3rd and 13th Armies and the newly formed 50th Army, to Block the Germans advance by filling the gap between the Western and Reserve Fronts and the weak Soviet Central Front.[45] This front with a total of fifteen rifle divisions, three cavalry divisions, one tank division and one airborne corps, was tasked with covering a front line of 230 kilometers from south of Roslavl in the north to Novgorod-Sversky in the south.[46]

    Colonel General Fyodor Kuznetsov, the commander of the Soviet Central Front, decided to abandon Gomel on August 15. The soviet rear guard left behind to protect the retreat of the rest of the Central Front forces caused problems for the Germans in Gomel. On August 16, Bock ordered the German Second Army to leave only the necessary forces in the Zhlobin area and with the rest of Its forces to attack at full speed towards Gomel in the south. However, the resistance and attacks of the Red Army in the Gomel region on the same day stopped the advance of the XLIII and XIII Army Corps of the German Second Army to the south. In order to prevent the escape of Soviet forces from Gomel area, Guderian ordered the XXIV Motorized Corps to Starodub, 120 km east of Gomel. the road junction in Meglin, 60 kilometers north of Starodub, was captured 3rd Panzer Division of this corps On the same day. The next day, despite enduring heavy pressure from the west, the German 10th motorized infantry and 3rd Panzer divisions cut the Gomel-Bryansk railway.[47]

    Guderian's Panzer forces and the infantry of the Second Army occupied Pochep, 60 km northeast of Starodub, and reached the outskirts of Gomel and Starodub by August 18, in order to cause the danger of breaking contact between Soviet 13th and 21st armies and a breach between Soviet central and reserve fronts.[45] On the same day, Stavka formed Soviet 40th Army under the command of Major General Kuzma Podlas, as part of the Southwestern Front, specifically to block Gudrian's Road.[44] This action was of little value because this army consisted of two rifle divisions and an airborne corps, which were severely weakened in the previous battles in Dnieper River area.[48] On August 19, some units of 3rd Panzer Division under heavy attacks from the west, were surrounded by Red Army forces near Oncha, 30 kilometers north of Starodub. As the situation became serious, some forward forces of the 3rd Panzer Division returned to Oncha by turning from Starodub. Despite the settlement of the situation in Oncha, the infantry of the German Second Army was still far away from this place due to the clashes in the Zhlobin area, and the Red Army still blocked the Meglin-Oncha road.[49]

    In this situation, the Wehrmacht's too long Supply system was not able to keep up with the advance up with Panzer forces in the bad roads of the region. Therefore, the lack of supplies also created severe disruption as much as the resistance of the Red Army hindered the advance of the German XXIV Motorized Corps. However, given the poor mechanical condition of the Panzer Divisions and the proneness of their tanks to break down, the biggest obstacle to Guderian's advance was the bad Soviet roads. The 60-kilometer Roslavl-Meglin road, the main north–south artery for Guderian, has been described as "very bad".[50] Thus, during this period, despite the order of the German Army High Command to XXIV Motorized Corps to advance towards Gomel from the east, this unit was unable to do so due to the pressure from the Red Army and logistical problems. The incoordination between the different German divisions created a difficult situation for the Germans in the Gomel region.[49] The Diaries of the logistics officer of 3rd Panzer Division on August 18 stated: "The advance of the division is so rapid that the [supply] columns are unable to keep themselves coordinated." The supply columns of the XXIV Motorized Corps were moving at an average speed of only 12 kilometers per hour. The next day, the lack of fuel forced Generel der Panzertruppen Leo Geyr von Schwepenburg, the commander of the XXIV Motorized Corps, to inform Guderian's headquarters that the capture of Novozibkov, 70 kilometers east of Gomel, was not feasible. Guderian, in turn, reported this issue to Bock. In addition, the lack of spare parts brought the number of operational tanks of the 4th Panzer Division to only 44, and on August 22 in 3rd Panzer Division to only 60 tanks.[51] Finally, on August 21, the fuel needs of the XXIV Motorized Corps were re-supplied with air transport and Starodub was taken by it on August 22.[52]

    Germans final attack on Gomel began on August 19 with XIII Army Corps attacking it from the northwest and northeast. 17th infantry division of this army penetrated into the city from the west and north on the same day, so that intense house-to-house clashes broke out. By evening, the Red Army troops were pushed to the southern part of the city. These forces tried their best to destroy all the bridges of the Sozh River in the city. The fighting continued for another day in Gomel.[53] Despite the strong resistance of the Soviet 21st Army, Panzer Group 2 and the German 2nd Army completely captured Gomel by August 21.[54] The cleaning of the city of last Red Army elements was done by the German 131st Infantry Division.[55] A third of the defenders of Gomel شقثش were encircled by Germans and destroyed.[56] About 50,000 soldiers of the Red Army were captured by the Germans in this position.[57] By fall of Gomel, the Soviet 5th Army, enduring heavy pressure in its northern flank By German 2nd Army, began to retreat eastward from the area north of Mosir, southwest of the Pripyat Marshes.[55]

    Gudrian's entry into Ukraine[edit]

    On August 21, Hitler ordered the all of the German Second Army and the Panzer Group 2 turn to the south, deep in Ukraine.[45] However, on August 23, the German Army High Command, in planning to pursue a simultaneous offensive towards the south and Moscow, splited the Panzer Group 2 and ordered XLVIII Motorized Corps to ber remained in the Smolensk region as a mobile reserve. On the same day, Guderian, without knowing about such an order, in his meeting with Hitler in Wolfschanze, received his promise about his Panzer group would remain united.[58] The next day, Guderian learned about OKH Order and immediately requested the return of the corps, which was scheduled to join the German 4th Army. However Guderian's protest was tured down by Bock.[59] In any case, Panzer Group 2 resumed its advance to the south on August 25, attacking the Soviet central front through Gomel and Starodub, driving it back in disarray towards the Desna River.[45]

    Desna River was considered a major natural obstacle in front of Guderian's forces on the way to enter Ukraine. On August 25, 3rd Panzer Division reached Novgorod-Sversky on the bank of this river. Panzer Group 2 initially received reports about the destruction of the big bridge on this river. However, later it was revealed that the 800-meter bridge, due to the incompetence of the Red Army forces and the quick action of the Germans, was captured intact by the 3rd Panzer Division on the morning of August 26, as a crossing over the Desna River. Guderian called this news "surprising and the most pleasant". After this success, 3rd Panzer Division expanded its hold to a depth of eight kilometers to protect the bridgehead. The next day, Soviet forces made a strong effort to take back the eastern bank of the river and destroy its bridge. The German bridgehead shrank under pressure, but Germans eventually repulsed the attacks. German 10th Motorized Infantry Division also captured another crossing on the Desna River near Kurup, 50 km southwest of Novgorod-Sversky, At the same time. On August 30, this division tried to continue advancing south alone, but directly hit into Yeryomenko's offensive arrow and repulsed. According to diaries of Bock and Guderian, this division was not even able to protect its bridgehead south of the Desna river under the attack of four enemy divisions.[60] Anyway, The erosion weakened 3rd Panzer Division and the 10th Motorized Infantry Division of the XXIV Motorized Corps to such an extent that these two divisions took a defensive position for a while.[41]

    Army Group South crossing of the Dnieper[edit]

    By the end of August, the Soviet Southwestern Front, consisting of more than 50 divisions in 6 armies, was completely situated in a triangular salient with its apex west of Kiev at a depth of 500 km.[61] The long flanks of this front were attacked from the north by German Panzer Armored Group 2 and the 2nd Army, from the west by the German 6th Army, and from the south by German Panzer Group 1 and 17th Army. Even before the start of the new German operation, Soviet Southwestern front was in a precarious position and already semi-encircled. The most powerful formations of Kirponos, being deployed in the western areas, were not facing the danger of the German Panzer forces on the outer flanks. Kirponos was aware of these conditions, but the approach of the Soviet High Command would not allow him to take effective action.[62]

    After Budyonny and Kirponos's repeated requests to Stavka to withdraw the Soviet Fifth Army to shorter lines, and the Soviet High Command's agreement to that on August 19, this army crossed the Dnieper to the east on August 23.[63] The command of the German 6th Army did not expect the withdrawal of the Soviet 5th Army until August 18. Halder and the command of the Army Group South ordered to quickly pursue the these soviet forces. Bad roads in the area delayed the German 6th Army.[64] In any case, the Soviet Fifth Army was not able to destroy the wooden bridge over the Dnieper behind itself. The bridge was Assaulted by LI Army Corps of the German 6th Army and 11th Panzer Division forces, and was taken over. Kirponos ordered all air Assets at his disposal to destroy the bridge. Soviet Il-2 aircraft destroyed the bridge with incendiary bombs. This cut the way behind the German troops who had crossed the Dnieper River. These forces were isolated from other German units in the marshes between the Dnieper and Desna for ten days until the German engineers repaired the bridge by September 2.[63]

    On August 26, the XVII Army Corps of the German 6th Army in the northern flank of the Army Group South captured Chernobyl, 110 km north of Kiev.[65] On September 1, Generalleutnant Georg von Sudenstern, the Chief of Staff of Army Group South, communicated with the Army Group Center's Second Army in order to coordinate his left wing. He immediately announced that due to the lack of equipment to cross the river, the 6th Army of the Army Group South is dependent on the help of the German 2nd Army.[66]

    Despite the difficulties and lack of supplies, the motorized corps of Panzer Group 1 began a period of several days rebuilt and rest from August 27 and 28. This was while XXIV Motorized Corps, as Gudrian's armored spearhead, in the northern pincers of the Wehrmacht operation in the Kiev region, after a continuous series of heavy battles and exhausting marches, without any opportunity to rest and repair, had begun a new operation on August 25.[67] However, due to the difficult geographical conditions east of the Dnieper River and north of Kiev, the strong resistance of the Soviet Fifth Army and the logistical problems of the Germans that slowed down the advance of the Second and Sixth Armies, Rundstedt was worried about the escape of the Soviet forces to the east. On August 28 and 29, he ordered the Panzer Group 1 and the 6th and 17th armies to cross the Dnieper River in as many places as possible.[68] On September 1, Panzer Group 1 received an order to end the rebuilt operation and return to the front line.[69] In fact, this meant that the command of Army Group South decided to use its worn-out forces with "the last of their remaining strength" and without rest.[29]

    Hence, LII Army Corps of the German 17th Army seized a crossing on the Dnieper in Drifka, south of Kremenchuk, 280 kilometers southeast of Kiev, on August 31. The Paznzer forces of the Army Group South expanded this bridgehead by re-entering the scene on September 4. Facing this danger, Marshal Budyonny immediately ordered Soviet 38th Army under the command of Major General Feklenko, to destroy the German forces that have crossed the river. However, this army was too weak to do so[70] And its planned counterattack on September 8, without armored forces, with little ammunition and under enemy air attacks in open space, never materialized.[71]

    The intensification of the soviet resistance and the belief in the transfer of powerful units of the Red Army from other parts of the front line to the region caused the Germans to change their assessment of the situation after the end of August and make it clear to them that Stalin has ordered to preserve the Dnieper river line. Initially, Rundstedt planned to send the 17th Army towards Guderian to the north through the Kremenchuk bridgehead, so the 1st Panzer Group would attack through the Dnipropetrovsk bridgehead to the east.[72] Accordingly, on September 4, Army Group South ordered the 17th Army to attack the Mirgorod-Lubny general line in order to encircle the enemy forces in the Middle Dnieper region and Kiev. Panzer Group 1 was to cover the right flank of this movement by advancing towards Poltava via Krasnograd in a northerly direction.[73] After a meeting on September 8 at the headquarters of Army Group South in presence of Brauchich and Halder, it was decided to reverse the roles of the 17th Army and the Panzer Group 1.[72] The purpose of this change was to complete the encirclement of the enemy as quickly as possible. It seems that the pressure of the Army Group Center and Panzer Group 2 Command, in bringing the Panzer forces of the Army Group South into the operation was effective in decision.[73] In addition, it was decided that the Panzer Group 1 would advance to Romny 200 km to the north and hold a 100 km line from Lubny in the south in order to block the path of possible strong enemy attacks from this area to open an escape route. Four infantry divisions of the 17th Army were also to hold the rest of the encirclement ring from Lubny to the south, while its other six infantry divisions were to move towards Kharkov in the east. The start date of the new operation was set on September 11. Before Halder left the meeting, Rundstedt asked him to put the Panzer Group 2 and the 2nd Army under his command in order to create unity in the command of operations in the Kiev area, which was rejected.[74]

    V Air Corps Luftwaffe was deployed to new bases in Kirovgrad and Uman to provided air support to Army Group South.[72] The LII, LV and parts of the XI Army Corps of the German 17th Army crossed the Dnieper in the center of the front line of the Southern Army Group until September 10. Panzer Group 1, led by the forces of Generalmajor Hans-Valentin Hube in the 16th Panzer Division of XLVIII Motorized Corps, began crossing the Dnieper on September 11.[70] by September 12, Army Group South concentrated 20 divisions in this area against 5 rifle and 4 cavalry divisions of the 38th Army in the southern flank of the soviet southwestern front.[75] Rain, muddy roads, logistical problems and local counter-attacks of the Red Army delayed the advance of the Army Group South to some extent. 16th Panzer Division as the armored spearhead of the XLVIII Motorized Corps initially achieved success, but running out of fuel, stopped to resupply.[76] At this time, Panzer Group 1 had a total of 331 tanks, which was equivalent to 53% of its initial strength on June 22.[72] After collecting the necessary supplies and building a suitable bridge, XLVIII Motorized Corps under the command of Lieutenant General Werner Kempf, resumed the attack.[77]

    Bryansk front counterattack[edit]

    Beyond the losses inflicted to the Red Army as a result of the Army Group Center actions in the northern (Velikye Luki area) and southern (Roslavl, Krychev, Zhlobin, and then Gomel) flanks, it seems that Stalin had made a wrong strategic assessment of the situation. He believed that these attacks were merely tactical measures to divert attention or resources from the vital Smolensk-Moscow axis.[78] Until this time, the Soviet senior commanders thought that Guderian's movement to the south objective was attacking to Bryansk and then Moscow. Therefore, the Bryansk Front was generally responsible for defending the Moscow axis from the southwest and was not responsible for preventing the penetration of the Panzer Group 2 in the northern flank of the soviet Southwestern Front. Moreover, by keeping troops in the Kiev area, the Soviet High Command probably hoped to use them against the southern flank of Army Group Center as it advanced towards Moscow.[79]

    However, contrary to the expectations of the Red Army, Guderian continued to attack to the south almost undisturbed, taking the enemy by surprise.[80] However, Stalin still insisted that Kirponos should continue to defend Kiev at any cost and believed that the Bryansk front, in addition to protecting the Bryansk axis, is also capable of defeating the enemy forces that are threatening the northern flank and behind the southwestern front and to destroy them as well.[81] In any case, Stalin refused to order an all-out attack by the Bryansk front against Guderian's eastern flank in order to preserve the forces of the front to stop the future advance of Army Group Center towards Moscow.[82]

    On August 25, Stavka ordered the Western Front under the command of General Semyon Timoshenko, the Reserve Front under the command of General Zhukov, and the Bryansk Front under the command of General Yeryomenko to carry out concentrated counter-attacks in the Smolensk-Yelnia-Novozibkov axis to repel Guderian's invasion and in the process to confront the entire Army Group Center.[83] Accordingly, after the dissolution of the soviet Central Front and addition of the Soviet 21st Army under its command, the Bryansk Front was obliged to defeat the Panzer Group 2 by conducting a direct attack and recover the strong defense of the north of Kiev. This operation was to take place on a very wide front line from Zhukovka in the north to Yampol in the south. On August 26, Stavka asked the Bryansk front to encircle and destroy Gudrian's forces in Starodub and bring the flanks of the soviet 13th and 21st armies closer to each other. This order was issued while the formation of the Bryansk Front was not yet completed, and its implementation would cause the use of the Red Army forces piecemeal.[84] Meanwhile, there was a large gap between the main part of the Bryansk Front and the reinforcements from the former Central Front, and only the Soviet 50th Army was fresh and capable of sustained offensive operations.[85] In any case, Stavka insisted on continuing these futile counterattacks.[86] False information led the Red Army to believe that the Germans were planning to inflict a heavy blow on the right (north) flank of the Bryansk front. Therefore, Shaposhnikov ordered Yeryomenko to reinforce this part with the 50th Army. This action caused this army to move from the threatened area to an area where there was no danger, and the counterattack of the Bryansk front was greatly weakened at the necessary point.[87]

    After the failure of the first counterattack of the Bryansk Front, on August 28, Stavka ordered a massive air attack by 450 aircraft of the Bryansk Front with at least 100 DB-3 bombers against the German tanks from dawn a day or two later.[86] Between August 29 and September 4, Soviet aircraft made more than 4,000 sorties against the Panzer Group 2. Despite Yeryomenko's post-war claims that this massive air offensive was "very effective",[88] these Soviet air attacks, although carried out according to the plan, had almost no effect on Guderian's advance.[86] During these attacks, 55 Soviet aircraft were shot down and 57 other aircraft were destroyed by German attacks on eight Soviet air bases.[88] Following this inconclusive air operation, two days later the Stavka again ordered the Bryansk Front, this time with all its forces, to launch another counterattack towards Roslavl and Starodub and destroy Guderian's forces at Pochep, Novgorod-Sversky and Novozibkov, and subsequently further advance to reach the Petrovich-Klimovichi-Schers line until September 15. It was clear that such an operation was far beyond the capabilities of this front with inexperienced forces and a severe lack of staff and command personnel. However, Yeryomenko ordered the counteroffensive to begin on September 2. The two shock groups of the Bryansk front had to operate in two axes with a distance of 140 kilometers between them.[89]

    Before that, Soviet 108th Tank Division attacked on August 30 by more than 90 tanks, including a large number of KV-1 heavy tanks, on which the usual German anti-tank weapons did not work, against the 17th Panzer Division of XLVII Motorized Corps in the eastern flank of Guderian. This attack a 2-kilometer breach in the German lines and raised the concern of splitting this German corps in half. 17th Panzer Division's counterattack next day was not effective due to the superiority of the Red Army tanks and resulted in the loss of 11 German tanks. On September 1, the counterattack of the 18th Panzer Division, another division of XLVII Motorized Corps, also collided with the Soviet 4th Cavalry Division, which was in the area in support of its tank division. Finally, with the fire concentration from two Panzer divisions and the air support of the II Air Corps of the Luftwaffe, the attack of Yeryomenko's tanks was repelled after causing great damage to both sides.[90]

    Due to the confusing operational conditions, the reorganization of the Bryansk front faced many supply problems and were not resolved until the beginning of the operation. Therefore, Yeryomenko asked for the postponement of the counterattack until September 3, which was agreed. However, under pressure from Stavka, Yeryomenko was again forced to launch the counterattack on September 2. After two hours of artillery shelling, both shock groups directly attacked Gudrian's advance forces. Inadequate air support, uncoordinated attacks and other factors caused this counterattack of the Bryansk front to fail from the very beginning.[89] XLVII Motorized Corps easily repelled these counterattacks. During these clashes, the armored forces of the Soviet Bryansk Front, despite their apparent strength, were quickly destroyed, and Guderian's tanks continued to move south.[91]

    After the war, Yeryomenko estimated this counterattack from operational point of view, very effective too, which has led to gaining a lot of experience for the Red Army. In any case, even though such claims were made to save face, if not for Stalin's insistence on prohibiting the withdrawal of the Soviet Southwestern Front, Yeryomenko's efforts and his counterattack probably could have provided enough time to pull out bulk of the Kirponos' forces.[88]

    The discussion about continuation of the Wehrmacht operation[edit]

    On August 26, Guderian asked again the German Army High Command to release the XLVI Motorized Corps, which was rejected again. On August 27, during visit of Major General Friedrich Paulus, Quartermeister of OKH, to the headquarters of the Panzer Group 2, Guderian again requested to receive more reinforcrement. Guderian stated that the German 2nd Army, advancing towards the southwest, is in an operationally diverging course from Panzer Group 2, and is now separated from the right wing of XXIV Motorized Corps which created a 75 km gap between them. He considered the left (eastern) wing of the XXIV Motorized Corps to be similarly insecure. Gudrian proposed the solution in transferring the XIII and XLIII Corps as well as the 1st Cavalry Division from the 2nd Army to his command. Guderian also repeated the request for the return of the XLVI motorized corps. Paulus agreed to support these requests. On the same day, Guderian contacted Generalmajor Hans von Greifenberg, the Chief of Staff of Army Group Center, and made a similar request for reinforcement. Considering the pressure exerted on the long defensive line of the Army Group Center and its need to have a mobile reserve, Bock, in consultation with Halder, initially agreed only to move the 1st Cavalry Division closer to the right flank of the XXIV Motorized Corps. When Guderian insisted on his demands again on August 29, Bock rejected Guderian's claims by stating that the XXIV Motorized Corps had informed the 2nd Army that it did not feel a threat on its right flank. However, bock agreed Grossdeutschland infantry regiment also to be transferred to Guderian the next day. Of course, this "drop-by-drop reinforcement" did not convince Gudrian.[92]

    On August 30, Operations department of the German Army General staff ordered Army Group Center to prepare to place the divisions of the 2nd Army under the command of Panzer Group 2 after the 2nd Army crossed on Desna river, "in order to continue operations with the Army Group South." The headquarters and "parts of the 2nd Army that are not needed in the continuation of operations south of the Desna River" remained at the disposal of the Army Group Center. The number of these "required divisions" depended on the demands of Army Group South. This decision marked confirmation that future operations would be led by Army Group South and would no longer simply be an intensified effort to eliminate enemy soviet between the two Army Groups, with Army Group Center in charge.[66]

    In the meantime, the issue of continuing the advance to the east towards Moscow seriously entered the speculations of the German high command. David Stahel, an American historian, raises these issues not as a plan to continue the operation after the completion of Guderian's current movement to the south, but as a serious consideration of the overall change of the operational plan by Hitler; The idea was that the Panzer Group 2 and the 2nd Army would stop at the Nagin-Konotop railway, before completing the encirclement of the Soviet Southwestern Front, and rush eastward with the rest of Army Group Center.[93] In this regard, after informing about the Army General staff directive, Hitler informed Brauchitsch on the same day, August 30, that the strength of the Army Group Center around Desna River should not be used for operations in Ukraine, but must be prepared to be used in direction of Moscow. Brian Fugate considers these forces mentioned by Hitler to be only the units of the XLVI Motorized Corps, which despite the promise of transferring them to Guderian, the fuehrer reconsidered their use.[94] Anyway, Halder Bock, who had stubbornly wanted to continue advance towards Moscow, now seeing the operational opportunities in the south, considering Guderian's movement as irreversible and considering the high attrition to his units and the bad supply situation, did not take a favorable view of such an action, which would have involved a large turn-around and a time-consuming and costly redeployment of forces, and strongly desired to complete the offensive to the south.[95] Hitler finally decided to allow Guderian to continue south and Connect to the Panzer Group 1.[96]

    The continuation of Gudrian's advance[edit]

    At this time, while XLVII Motorized Corps was solely responsible for protecting the long left (eastern) flank of the Panzer Group 2, only 86 operational tanks, equivalent to less than half of the tanks of 3rd Panzer Division at the beginning of the Barbarossa operation, had left for XXIV Motorized Corps as Gudrian's armored spearhead. In this way, Gudrian's attack had to proceed with a number of tanks equivalent to half an Panzer division.[97] Only 4th Panzer division of this corps was capable of offensive operations at the time.[41] Therefore, that same evening, Bock reluctantly agreed to send SS Panzer Division Das Reich to Guderian. According to -Bock, in a report on September 2, Guderian described the situation of the Panzer Group 2 so pessimistic that the commander of the army group proposed to get permission for the tactical withdrawal of Gudrian's forces from OKH, so that these forces can support the German 2nd army on their left flank. Anyway. Guderian immediately rejected this proposal and used the opportunity to insist on his previous request. He considered the Panzer Group 2 capable of accomplishing its mission if it received more reinforcements. Finally, under the influence of Guderian's report, Bock agreed to release XLVI Motorized Corps on September 2.[98]

    After four days of Halting armored spearhead at the Desna river crossing and receiving sufficient supplies, the advance of the Panzer Group 2 to the south was resumed on August 31.[41] Guderian's offensive pushed aside the Bryansk front and put the Soviet 180th tank division in a semi-encirclement situation at the end of September 2 and dealt a heavy blow to its 4th cavalry division. Until September 6, the mobile forces of the Bryansk front were surrounded and fighting for their survival.[99] Guderian's forces had captured about 30,000 soviet forces until the tenth day of their advance in Ukraine.[100]

    Stalin severely criticized Yeryomenko, and allowed to pull his troops out of the encirclement if possible, and for this purpose, use his air power even in bad weather. At this time, only 16 tanks and 5 armored vehicles left for the Soviet 108th tank division, and only 38 tanks left for the 141 tank brigade. All of Yeryomenko's actions in this direction, including the use of roadblocking units that opened fire on his own forces that were retreating without permission, did not achieved any results.[101]

    In a report to Stalin on September 4, Budyonny described the threat in his flanks and requested immediate reinforcements. In the absence of this possibility, Budyonny requested permission to create a reserve force for himself by transferring four divisions from the 37th and 26th armies of the Western Front. At Stalin's behest, Shaposhnikov rejected both of Budyonny's requests.[102] On September 6, when the soviet 21st Army was placed under the command of the Southwest Front, Kirponos ordered this army to attack the rear of the 3rd and 4th Panzer Divisions XXIV Motorized Corps from the west. The actions of Kirponos did not affect the movement of Gudrian's armored forces and they crossed the Saim river the next day. Faced with a dangerous situation, on September 7, Kirponos asked for permission to withdraw his forces in north of Kiev to the eastern side of the Desna River.[103] After the fall of Chernigov[b] On September 8 to German 2nd Army, Shaposhnikov informed the Southwestern Front on September 9 that the High Command had decided to withdraw the Fifth Army and the right wing of the Soviet 37th Army to the east of the Desna River in order to take a distance from the German Sixth Army and rotate its front line to the north, facing the advance of the Panzer Group 2.[105]

    Sacrificing soviet Southwestern Front[edit]

    While the Soviet Southwestern Front was defending a front line of more than 800 kilometers, on September 11, Budyonny sent a telegram to Stalin stating that only powerful forces would be able to prevent the encirclement of this front; While the southwestern front itself does not have such a possibility. He announced that if the soviet high command does not provide the necessary reserve forces in this area, it is necessary to pull back the southwestern front[106] And the delay in this matter will only cause the loss of its forces and the destruction of a large amount of supply units.[107] Thus, He requested permission to withdraw 250 kilometers eastward from the Dnieper River to the Pasel River.[35] However, Stalin again ordered Kirponos not to abandon Kiev without Stavka's permission and not to destroy the bridges.[107] Stalin believed that if the Southwestern Front retreats from the Dnieper River line, the Germans will quickly gain a foothold on its eastern bank; As a result, the southwest front will be under the pressure of the enemy from three directions instead of two when retreating, and finally, if the Germans move coordinated, it will be surrounded; Because it is not possible to withdraw in time the entire front with 667,000 troops to behind the Pasel River. He considered the encirclement of the 6th and 12th Soviet armies in Uman as the result of such a retreat that should not be repeated again. Stalin also did not consider the defense of Pasel River ready to use. [108] In any case, even from Germans point of view, the initial stages of the operation in Ukraine were still by no means a guarantee of ultimate success.[109]

    At suggestion of Zhukov and by order of Stalin[110] On September 13, Stavka replaced desperate Budyonny by Marshal Semyon Timoshenko in command of the Soviet Southwestern direction without issuing a withdrawal authorization.[107] Timoshenko, believing that reinforcements were on the way and the Bryansk Front's counteroffensive would have good results, agreed to the Southwestern Front not to retreat. However, only one rifle division and two tank brigades with 100 tanks were provided to him, and it was clear that the Bryansk front could not do anything with only 20 operational tanks in all its tank brigades.[111]

    Like what happened to the Soviet Western Front in the Battle of Białystok–Minsk, anticipating the Wehrmacht's follow-up offensive by moving towards the Soviet capital, it seems that the main intention of the Soviet leader was to sacrifice the Southwestern Front of the Red Army in Kiev in order to buy time to strengthen Moscow's defenses, And deployment of strategic reserves around it. The Soviet Southwestern Front would have to stand and fight and be destroyed to waste the Germans' time and resources. Just as Dmitri Pavlov, the commander of the Soviet Western Front, was not aware of such a plan, Kirponos and Yeryomenko were also unaware of what was going on in Stalin's head. Due to their close position to Stalin, Budyonny and Zhukov probably knew about such a plan. Thinking that after transfer of Panzer Group 2 to the Army Group South, the Germans will continue to move in the south and advance to the Volga River and the Caucasus without any problems, they were trying to withdraw the southwestern front to the east to save it and stop the Germans. Therefore, changing of their positions can be related to this.[112]

    Anyway, other purposes of Stalin in insisting on resistance in the Kiev region are to preserve the vital agricultural lands of Ukraine in order to prevent social chaos resulting from the loss of food resources, to encourage the defenders of Leningrad and Odessa to continue resistance and to reassure the United Kingdom and the United State, supply-armament supporters of the Soviet Union, of its endurance against German invaders.[113]

    Encirclement completion[edit]

    In this situation, on September 12, by transferring tank units from the 55th Army to the 13th Army, Stavka in an optimistic order, by changing the attack plan of the Bryansk Front, ordered Yeryomenko to stop his offensive along the Roslavl axis, and by reorganizing his forces, target Guderian's left wing once again to cover the created gap by September 18 and connect with the units of the southwestern front. To encourage Yeryomenko, Stavka promoted him to the rank of Colonel General and assigned some reinforcement to the disorganized Bryansk Front. These arrangements were made very late.[107] The same day with a one-day delay due to raining,[114] 16th Panzer Division of Army Group Center, by crushing the defense of the Soviet 297th Rifle Division, moved 20 kilometers from the Dnieper River bridgehead to the north and reached Khorol, 230 kilometers southeast of Kiev, by evening.[107] The division captured the headquarters of the Soviet 38th Army, but Feklenko, the commander of this army, managed to escape.[115] The next day, 14th Panzer Division of Army Group Center reached Lubni, 30 kilometers north of Khorol. By attacking the supply line of the Soviet Southwest Front, this division captured 1,500 soldiers, 600 trucks, 70 cannons, and 3 aircraft.[116] 9th Panzer Division was chasing these two divisions. By this time, with this attack, Kleist's forces in the Panzer Group 1 created a gap of 20 kilometers between the Soviet 38th and 6th Armies, and were only 40 kilometers away from Generalmajot Walther Model's forces in the 3rd Panzer Division of the Army Group Center in the north.[117] At the same time, the resistance of the Red Army forces was increasing and a fierce air battle was going on in the sky of the region between the fighters of two sides. The 16th Panzer Division met the stubborn resistance of the NKVD elements in Lubny, and the passage of 3rd Panzer Division from Romny lasted two days.[118] These problems caused the German Army High Command plan to connect two Panzer groups to one another on September 13 not to be realized. In any case, a small battle group from the 3rd Panzer Division, consisting of three tanks, eight armored reconnaissance vehicles, six artillery pieces, and an anti-tank company, stormed southward and captured the important city of Lukhvitsa and its bridge over the Sula River. Due to the lack of strength in this situation, Guderian requested infantry take place of the 18th Panzer Division, which was currently was holding a 90-kilometer line, in the northern part of his eastern flank, so that the division could move towards Romny. The command of the army group opposed this request due to the long distance of this division from the intended target and also the attrition that was brought to it before the upcoming attack towards Moscow.[119]

    In the early morning of September 14, Major General Vasily Tupikov, the Chief of Staff of the Soviet Southwestern Front, sent a personal telegram to Shaposhnikov and stated: "The disaster has begun and will show itself to you in a few days." In a sharp response, Shaposhnikov described Tupikov as a "panicky" and called for "calmness and not to yield to panic" at all levels of command and to stop the retreat of the Soviet 21st and 5th Armies. He asked the forces of the Soviet Southwestern Front "not look backwards" and to "fulfill comrade Stalin's order of September ll".[120] On September 15, under pressure, Timoshenko completely reversed his previous opinion and told Shaposhnikov in Moscow that he wanted the immediate withdrawal of the Soviet Southwestern Front.[121] Meanwhile, with the pressure of the German 6th and 2nd armies, the northern wing of the Soviet southwestern front was gradually collapsing. German 17th army also slowly began to advance from the Kremenchuk bridgehead to the north. XI Army Corps of the Germnan 17th Army with three infantry divisions was placed under the command of the Panzer Group 1 on September 13 to ensure the Protection of the eastern wall of the encirclement that was being completed.[122]

    Finally, at 18:00 on September 14, a small battle group of 18th Panzer Division reached an engineer company of 16th Panzer Division north of Lubny. However, this meeting between the first vanguard elements of the northern and southern pincers was symbolic, and there was still a relatively large space between the two pincers. The next day, more units of the 9th Panzer Division continued moving north to establish a stronger connection.[123] On September 16, with the complete connection of the 3rd and 16th Panzer Divisions, the armored spearheads of the Panzer Groups 1 and 2, in the south of Lukhvitsa, the encirclement ring was completed 120 kilometers southeast of Kiev, so all the Soviet Southwestern Front, including the five Armies, 5th, 21st, 26th, 37th and 38th, were traped inside it. The encirclement ring was formed on the eastern side by the Panzer groups 1 and 2 and on the western side by the German 2nd and 6th armies.[124] The encirclement included a large region with an area of approximately 20,000 square kilometers, 200 kilometers from the connection point of the Wehrmacht's Panzer forces to the city of Kiev, east to west.[125]

    Southwestern front attempt to retreat[edit]

    On the same day, September 16, in a meeting with General Bagramian, the chief of operations of the Southwestern Front, in Poltava, where Khrushchev was also present, Timoshenko stated that when the encirclement ring of the Germans is not yet tight, a decision should be made without delay to allow the Southwestern Front to retreat. Timoshenko Claimed that the High Command will eventually make this decision, but there is no time to waste in receiving confirmation from Moscow. According to Bagramian's memoirs, taking into account the tone of Shaposhnikov's previous telegram, Timoshenko himself deeply doubted the truth of this opinion. Timoshenko ordered Bagramian to fly to Priyatin and verbally order Kirponos to "abandon the Kiev fortification area and, leaving the covering forces on the Dnieper River, immediately begin withdrawing the main body of forces to the defensive rear line (Psel River).[c] Kirponos should also carry out counter-attacks in Lubny and Rumny areas to slow down the Panzers as much as possible. Timoshenko, who basically did not have the authority to issue such an order contrary to Stalin's wishes, refused to give Bagramian a written document in this regard, under the pretext that this flight could be too dangerous and such important orders should not fall into the hands of the enemy. In any case, leaving a written trace of such disobedience to Stalin could have cost both their lives. However, after Bagramian delivered Timoshenko's verbal order to the commander of the Soviet Southwestern Front on September 17, Kirponos avoided implementing it until reception of written confirmation. Ignoring Bagramian's protests, he decided to send a telegram to Moscow and receive the confirmation.[127]

    On the same day, Shaposhnikov and Vasilevsky "in a difficult conversation" were once again trying to convince Stalin to withdraw Kirponos' forces; Nevertheless, the Soviet leader, getting angry and accusing the commanders of the Red Army of "weak resistance against the enemy", agreed only with the withdrawal of the 5th and 37th armies to better defensive positions. Despite the breakdown in communications, Kirponos finally received a withdrawal confirmation from Shaposhnikov on the night of September 17, but he was only allowed to leave Kiev without retreating to the Psel river. However, a few hours before the arrival of this order, Kirponos, on his own initiative, ordered the Soviet 5th, 21st and 37th armies to attack to the east in order to break the Germans armored wall. The 38th and 40th armies, from outside the encircled area, were also tasked to protect the flanks by attacking Rumny and Lubny in the north and south, respectively. However, due to being under attack from all directions, it was not possible to carry out a coordinated retreat.[128]

    Many of the Red Army forces efforts of the to break out of encirclement were made in a chaotic and unplanned manner. Most of these efforts had no organization and were accompanied with heavy casualties. The attempt to break the encirclement was generally made by the mass of infantry with little artillery support. Wave after wave of Red Army soldiers stormed the German lines incoherently, rarely concentrating on one point. However, the numerical superiority was often high enough and their efforts were not always fruitless. The attack of the Red Army forces from the east on September 18 partially split the German defense and reached one kilometer from Rumny. At this time, Rumny was the headquarters of the Panzer Group 2. Against the attack of two tank brigades, two rifle divisions and one or two cavalry divisions of the Red Army, Guderian had only two battalions of the 10th Motorized Infantry Division and a few anti-aircraft guns. The Soviet Air Force gained local superiority at this point and targeted Rumny with its heavy bombers. In this situation, Guderian requested help from the 14th Division of the Panzer Group 1 and ordered the transfer of a part of the SS Das Reich and 4th Panzer divisions from the inner ring to this point. The arrival of the SS division on September 19 was enough to save Rumny. 4th Panzer launched a counterattack from the south. Meanwhile, Gudrian moved his headquarters to Konotop. At this time, the encircled forces in the east of Piryatin launched a powerful attack against the positions of the German 35th Infantry Regiment of the 25th Infantry Division and by defeating them, temporarily broke the enemy's line. In any case, the Pamzer regiment of the 9th Panzer Division covered this breach with a successful counterattack. On the morning of September 21, the attacks of the Red Army against the center of the 239th Infantry Division of the German XI Army Corps front line, not only caused a breach in depth, but also surrounded the its 372nd Infantry Regiment. Lieutenant General Joachim von Kurtzfleisch, commander of the XI Army Corps, ordered a small force to be sent to this point from three other directions. At the same time, another breakthrough appeared, this time on the left flank of the German 125th division of this Corps. These breaches caused the front line of the units to be mixed up and created a confused and scattered situation between the forces of the two opposing forces; In a way, a German soldier wrote in his memoirs: "Often one does not understand who is surrounded; Bolsheviks or us!" The German effort to destroy the enemy elements in this area lasted until September 23.[129]

    Destruction of the pocket[edit]

    On September 1, the Soviet Southwestern Front had 850,000 troops (including 90,000 reserves and rearguard units), 3,923 cannons and mortars, 114 tanks and 167 combat aircraft. The area included 452,700 troops, 2,642 cannons and mortars, and 64 tanks.[130] The Luftwaffe had absolute air superiority over the region by employing the II and V Air Corps. Despite some supply problems, the Luftwaffe's air attacks caused a lot of damage to the encircled soviet forces and disrupted their movements. The Luftwaffe 5th Air Corps carried out more than 1,400 sorties and dropped about 570,000 tons of bombs on the enemy Between September 12 and 21, suffering only 27 casualties and 17 aircraft was shot down, causing the destruction of nearly 2,200 motor vehicles and 100 Soviet airvrafts.[131]

    The Germans divided the encircled forces of the Soviet Southwestern Front, which had suffered widespread casualties and had exhausted their supplies, into small isolated parts and destroyed one by one by tightening the ring.[130] The advance of the German 17th army to the east increased the distance between the besieged forces of the Soviet Southwestern Front and the rest of the Red army. XLVIII Motorized Corps and 11th Panzer Group had not only kept the southern part of the eastern wall of the pocket, but also made the area more compact by attacking from the west. At the same time, the LI Army Corps of the German 6th army made a significant advance to the south against the scattered resistance of the northern flank of Kirponos. This Corps linked up with elements of the XXXIV Army Corps, which had gained a new bridgehead at Rezhishchev, south of Kiev, to cut the encircled area in half. The actions of the Germans caused great confusion among the forces of the Soviet Southwestern Front. The disconnection between the Red Army units deprived it of the ability to coordinate and send orders. For the Soviet Southwestern Front's forces, this conflict was more than a battle, like a desperate struggle to save themselves. On September 18, the Soviet 21st Army headquarters personnel, including three generals, were captured by the 4th Panzer Division. The next day, the pocket was divided into three parts. The most powerful part around Boryspol was south-east of Kiev, which consisted mainly of troops of the Soviet 37th Army. The other two major areas were a bubble-like area west of Urzhitsa with the forces of the Soviet 26th Army, and the area between Priatin and Lukhvitsa with the remnants of the Soviet 5th and 26th Armies. Kirponus was in the third part. The area of the Soviet 5th and 21st armies was cleared by September 23.[132] The resistance of the remnants of the Soviet 26th Army in the Orzhitsa region, as the last part, eliminated by September 26.[130] Small groups of Red Army soldiers were still scattered over a wide area. These troops tried to escape in different ways, including in civilian clothes.[133] Only about 15,000 managed to get out of the pocket by October 2.[130] Among these were Bagramian, Vlasov, the commander of the 37th Army, Kostenko, the commander of the 26th Army, and Kuznetsov, the commander of the 21st Army.[134]

    In addition to tactical and operational issues, for the security of the forces inside the city, the command of the German 6th Army decided that until the surrounding area of the Kiev is completely cleared of any form of resistance, the German troops are only allowed to stay in it with the written permission of its headquarters. At the same time, a decree was issued in connection with the control of depots and maintaining order in Kiev.[77] The battle for the city itself began on September 16 with assault of the Generalleutnant Hans von Obstfelder XXIX Army Corps of the German 6th Army. The 71st and 296th infantry divisions of the were leading the attackers to passing through the strong Soviet defensive positions. The 95th Infantry Division and the 3rd Battalion of the 77th Sturmgeschütz Regiment also participated in clearing the fortifications. Inside the city, the Soviet government tried to encourage the defenders to continue their resistance by broadcasting Stalin's speeches through loudspeakers.[135] Anyway, Kiev was captured by German forces on September 19[136] but the clashes over this city continued until September 24.[135] Previously, all important materials were evacuated from Kiev and the railway lines were destroyed.[137]

    On September 19, the German 99th Light Division discovered explosive traps that had been placed in large buildings suitable for deploying troops and headquarters. An extensive and somewhat successful search began, but on September 24, an explosion next to the main post office in a captured weapons and ammunition depot started a large fire that quickly spread. The firefighters of the Sachsen regiment was not able to extinguish the fire. In order to contain the fire, the 99th sapping Battalion and the demolition squads of the 71st and 99th Infantry Divisions cleared a large area as a fire break . The big fire was finally extinguished on September 29 with the addition of German soldiers, emergency units and other local and German firefighters.[137] About 200 Germans died in the initial explosions or subsequent fires, and 10,000 to 25,000 civilians were left homeless.[138] "Partisans and Jews" were accused to be responsible of these events. The headquarters of the 195th military administration was removed from Kiev and the responsibility of city administration was entrusted to the commander of the 113th infantry division.[137] Following these events, a two-day massacre of 33,000 Jews in Babi Yar took place.[139]

    As a result of the battle in the Kiev region, four Soviet field armies including the 5th, 21st, 26th, and 37th armies with 43 divisions were completely destroyed.[140] By clearing this area until the end of September, according to official German reports, about 665,000 Red Army troops were captured and 824 tanks, 418 anti-tank guns, and 3,000 artillery pieces were destroyed.[141] However, the official history of the Soviet Union admits that the southwestern front had suffered heavy losses before the battle of Kiev, and claims that it is unlikely that the number of prisoners was more than 222,000.[142] On the other hand, there is no clear Stats on the amount of casualties of the German units involved in the battle of Kiev, but the quoted reports indicate that it was bloody for the Wehrmacht.[134]

    The aerial blockade of the region was not completed and a number of senior Soviet commanders, including Budyonny, Tهmoshenko and Khrushchev, were able to escape by aircraft. By the evening of September 20, Kirponos, together with the forces of the Soviet 5th Army, was able to reach Driukovshchyna, 15 kilometers southwest of Lukhovitsa. The Red Army column was attacked by the 3rd Panzer Division in this position. The Germans captured General Sotensky, the artillery commander of the Soviet 5th Army, and drove the enemy forces to the Shumikovo forests. Kirponos, Potapov, the commander of the soviet Fifth Army, their staff officers and about 2,000 Soviet troops continued to fight for several more hours. [140] Kirponos was leading his troops near the front line when he was seriously wounded in the left leg and taken deep into the forest. Shortly afterwards a mortar shell landed near him which led to his death.[144] With Kirponos killed, the remaining Soviet elements surrendered.[140] Mykhailo Burmystenko, the commissar of the Soviet Southwestern Front, a member of the Central Committee of the Soviet Union Communist Party and the second secretary of the Communist Party of Ukraine, who died in this battle, was the highest-ranking Soviet communist leader who was killed during World War II.[citation needed]

    Consequences and speculations[edit]

    The battle of Kiev can be considered an unprecedented defeat for the Red Army, which caused more damage than the battles of Minsk, Smolensk and other places. The counter-attacks of the Soviet Bryansk front to prevent these incidents did not bear fruit and caused the loss of about 100,000 soldiers and 140 tanks of the front out of initial a total of 260,000 soldiers and 260 tanks. After these defeats, the Bryansk front was in a chaotic state, and less than three weeks later, when the Wehrmacht's concentrated offensive towards Moscow began, it had only 200,000 troops to face it. With the Southwestern Front completely destroyed, the Soviets were forced to rebuild it from scratch[145] and finally, in order to fill the gap created in the south of the front line, by weakening Moscow's axis, transfer its forces from the central sector.[146]

    The victory in Kiev was considered one of the prerequisites for the advance of the Army Group Center, so that the German Army High Command and the Wehrmacht High Command (OKW), by returning their focus to the central part of the front, would resume the offensive in it.[141] Before this victory, due to the problems that arose and the German forces being caught in attrition battles in many places, including Smolensk, pessimism about the outcome of the operation on the eastern front had arisen in the minds of the Germans. With the success in the Kiev region, new hope was immediately created among the Germans about the end of the war on this front.[147]

    In any case, battle of Kiev brought positive results for the Soviet Union. Panzer Group 2 southward turn to encircle the Soviet Southwestern Front delayed the German advance toward Moscow for a month, and this eventually dragged the Wehrmacht's operations into winter which would be costly. However, on the other hand, it can be acknowledged that Germans destroyed many Soviet forces in Ukraine, which the Red Army could later use in defense of Moscow, in addition to dominating the vital economic lands of the Soviet Union, by securing the southern flank of the Army Group Center and It dealt a heavy blow to the Soviet Bryansk Axis forces, making their path towards Moscow smoother.[148] Zhukov says about this:

    We can imagine that without operation [in the direction of Ukraine], the situation of the German Army Group Center could have been even worse than It was found to be. Reserve forces of the [Soviet] High Command, which had been used in September to fill the gaps in the southwestern sector, could have been used in an attack on the flank and rear of the centeral group of the German armies advancing on Moscow.[149]

    On the other hand, the autumn rainy season and muddy roads (Rasputitsa) in Ukraine started in mid-September, which was one month earlier than the rainy season in Moscow, and the Wehrmacht, by transferring forces from the center of the front line to its south, continued the operations in this key area before facing the rainy season.[150] The destruction of the Soviet Southwestern Front completely opened the way for Army Group South to advance to the east of Ukraine without leaving any resistance force.[151] In addition, the large amount of equipment captured in the Kiev region enabled the Wehrmacht to increase the mobility of its units. Beyond the operational issues, in the strategic dimension, it was thought that Germany might be able to open Turkey's feet to the war in her side and convince Finland to participate more.[152] In this way, Germany could also put Iran under pressure in the south, for which Britain and the Soviet Union had plans.[153] Finally, during the eight weeks that the Battle of Kiev was going on, Army Group Center had the opportunity to recover and strengthen its worn-out forces and improve their supply situation, and also its infantry divisions were able to join the motorized forces at the front line in a relatively slow march to the east.[citation needed]

    The damage caused to the German forces as a result of the explosive traps planted in the city of Kiev by the Soviets caused a change in the Wehrmacht's approach on how to capture large cities in the continuation of operation Barbarossa.[135] Hitler forbade accepting such risks again and conquering large fortified cities with direct attacks, especially without Panzer units; Rather, it was decided that these cities should be left behind and surrounded and brought to their knees by artillery fire and air attacks. During the final attack on Moscow, on October 7, Hitler included Moscow and Leningrad in this and prohibited the entry of troops into these cities.[137]

    The encirclement of the Kiev region was the last successful major breakthrough of the Wehrmacht's Panzer forces over a long distance that the Germans were able to carry out in the Soviet Union.[154] The passage of time showed that the operational results of the Kiev battle, no matter how great they were, could not open the way for Germany in the strategic dimension.[155]
